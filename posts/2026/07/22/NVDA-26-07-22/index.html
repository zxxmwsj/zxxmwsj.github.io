NVIDIA (NVDA) Research Report

Research cutoff: 2026-07-22. Latest complete market close used: 2026-07-21.
Price: $207.29. Market capitalization: $5.0207 trillion. Enterprise value: $4.9803 trillion.
Current valuation: 31.75x trailing P/E, 19.81x P/S, 42.16x P/FCF, 41.82x EV/FCF.
One-sentence thesis: NVIDIA is a rare full-stack platform whose CUDA ecosystem, systems integration, networking, and execution have created extraordinary economics; however, at $207.29 the market already requires roughly 21% annual FCF growth for five years, leaving little room for a normal semiconductor cycle, customer insourcing, export controls, or margin normalization.

AI research bias awareness

Information-richness rating: A. NVIDIA has audited filings, frequent earnings disclosures, detailed segment data, and extensive third-party coverage. The main research trap is consensus reinforcement: a large amount of information can make the AI story feel more certain than the underlying 5–10 year economics are.

I have therefore emphasized inverse thinking: customer concentration, custom ASIC substitution, inventory commitments, export controls, the durability of 70%+ gross margins, and the price paid. Public data is strong for historical accounting and current disclosures, but much weaker for future AI utilization, customer return on invested capital, and the share of workloads that migrate to custom silicon.

The FY22–FY26 compound annual growth rates are 68.30% for revenue, 89.83% for operating income, 87.32% for net income, and 85.69% for FCF. FY24–FY26 audited figures are confirmed in NVIDIA’s FY26 Form 10-K; the five-year series is cross-checked against StockAnalysis financials.

Net income benefited from $15.929B of other income, primarily investment-related gains; operating income and FCF are better measures of recurring operating power.

2. Business mechanics and mix

The business essence is: customers pay NVIDIA for a rapidly improving, integrated computing platform—chips, systems, networking, and software—that reduces the cost and time of building AI capacity, while CUDA makes switching economically painful.

Segment and end-market mix

FY26 market Revenue % of total
Data Center 193,737M 89.72%
— Compute 162,361M 75.19%
— Networking 31,376M 14.53%
Gaming 16,042M 7.43%

Automotive 2,349M 1.09%
OEM and other 619M 0.29%

The two reported segments were Compute & Networking: $193.479B, 89.60%, and Graphics: $22.459B, 10.40%. Q1 FY27 was even more concentrated: Compute & Networking was $74.550B, 91.34%, and Graphics was $7.065B, 8.66%. The company’s FY26 10-K end-market table and Q1 FY27 segment disclosure support these figures.

Geography and customer concentration

FY26 revenue by direct-customer headquarters was:

Geography Revenue % of total
United States 149,617M 69.29%
Taiwan 42,345M 19.61%
China, including Hong Kong 19,677M 9.11%
Other 4,299M 1.99%

Revenue outside the United States was 31% in FY26, down from 41% in FY25. This is a headquarters-based measure; the end customer and shipping destination may differ. NVIDIA estimates that 76% of Data Center revenue from Taiwan-headquartered customers was attributable to end customers in the United States and Europe.

Customer concentration is material. In FY26, two direct customers represented 22% and 14% of total revenue. In Q1 FY27, three direct customers represented 21%, 17%, and 16%—54% combined—primarily in Compute & Networking. Three direct customers also represented 30%, 18%, and 16% of Q1 accounts receivable. This is a genuine risk even if the customers are financially strong: a small number of capital-allocation decisions can move NVIDIA’s revenue, inventory, and receivables simultaneously.

3. Moat assessment

Moat source Evidence Five-year direction Score
Technology and software CUDA, CUDA-X, libraries, SDKs, models, and more than 7.5M developers; over half of engineers work on software Wider, but must keep pace with open standards 5/5
Switching costs Porting production AI systems, kernels, models, and operations to another stack is costly and risky Still widening, though inference workloads are more modular 4.5/5
Scale and systems integration GPU, CPU, DPU, NVLink, InfiniBand/Ethernet, racks, and software co-designed as one platform Wider through Blackwell and networking 5/5
Brand and access Available through major server makers, cloud providers, and system integrators Wider 4/5
Pricing power 71.1% FY26 GAAP gross margin despite full-scale systems transition and inventory provisions Strong but not invulnerable 4/5
Network effects Developer ecosystem and installed base increase platform value Wider 4.5/5

The strongest moat is not the GPU alone. It is the accumulated software, developer familiarity, optimized libraries, system-level know-how, and supply-chain execution. NVIDIA’s 10-K describes CUDA, the ecosystem, and the full-stack architecture.

The moat can be damaged by three mechanisms: (1) inference becoming standardized enough that CUDA switching costs fall; (2) hyperscalers shifting a larger portion of workloads to custom ASICs; and (3) a rival achieving acceptable total cost of ownership while NVIDIA’s premium pricing compresses. The company itself identifies AMD, cloud companies’ internal AI teams, Broadcom, Marvell, and other networking/accelerator vendors as competitors in its competition disclosure.

Buffett question: Will the moat still be visible in 2036 if the market’s dominant workload shifts from model training to lower-cost, highly optimized inference?

4. Cash generation, working capital, and balance-sheet quality

Cash flow and reinvestment

Fiscal year OCF Capex FCF FCF margin SBC
FY22 9,108M 976M 8,132M 30.21% 2,004M
FY23 5,641M 1,833M 3,808M 14.12% 2,709M
FY24 28,090M 1,069M 27,021M 44.35% 3,549M
FY25 64,089M 3,236M 60,853M 46.63% 4,737M
FY26 102,718M 6,042M 96,676M 44.77% 6,386M

FY26 FCF was $96.676B, or 44.77% of revenue. Capex was only 6.25% of FCF, but NVIDIA remains economically dependent on suppliers’ capacity investments, advanced packaging, HBM availability, and customer data-center capex. Low corporate capex does not mean low ecosystem capital intensity.

FY26 operating working-capital changes consumed cash: receivables −$15.399B and inventories −$11.324B, partly offset by payables and accrued liabilities. In Q1 FY27, accounts receivable rose from $38.466B to $40.710B (+5.83%) and inventory rose from $21.403B to $25.797B (+20.53%). Q1 inventory provisions were $0.8B, versus $2.3B in Q1 FY26; FY26 provisions and excess-purchase obligations totaled $7.2B, with $1.5B of releases, producing a 2.6-point unfavorable gross-margin effect.

Balance sheet and capital allocation

At April 26, 2026:

  • Cash and equivalents: $13.237B.
  • Marketable debt securities: $37.098B.
  • Marketable equity securities: $30.237B.
  • Cash plus marketable securities: $80.572B.
  • Accounts receivable: $40.710B.
  • Inventory: $25.797B.
  • Total current assets: $150.995B.
  • Total current liabilities: $43.884B.
  • Working capital: $150.995B − $43.884B = $107.111B.
  • Current ratio: $150.995B ÷ $43.884B = 3.4408x.
  • SEC debt excluding leases: $1.000B current + $7.470B long-term = $8.470B.

For valuation I use conservative broad debt of $12.348B, matching StockAnalysis’ inclusion of lease liabilities. That gives net cash of $80.572B − $12.348B = $68.224B, excluding non-marketable equity investments. This is conservative because NVIDIA also held $43.364B of non-marketable securities, which I assign no value to.

FY26 share repurchases were $40.086B cash, against $96.676B FCF; the company repurchased 282M shares. Q1 FY27 repurchases were $19.312B cash for 108.3M shares. NVIDIA also increased its additional repurchase authorization by $80B in May 2026 and increased its quarterly dividend from $0.01 to $0.25 per share. The FY26 10-K capital-return disclosure and Q1 FY27 10-Q document these actions.

Stock-based compensation was $6.386B in FY26, equal to 5.32% of GAAP net income and 2.96% of revenue. It is not a cash expense, but it is an economic cost when measured against the shares that must be issued or repurchased. Net shares outstanding declined about 1.1% year over year, so buybacks have more than offset dilution to date.

Founder alignment is meaningful but not controlling: the 2026 proxy reports Jensen Huang beneficially owning 870.6M shares, or 3.58%, as of March 23, 2026. His incentives are strongly aligned with long-term value, but key-person and succession risk remain relevant because NVIDIA’s product cadence and ecosystem strategy are unusually founder-shaped.

ROE and ROA

Using average FY25–FY26 balance-sheet denominators from the SEC filings:

  • ROE = 120.067 ÷ ((157.293 + 79.327) ÷ 2) = 101.49%.
  • ROA = 120.067 ÷ ((206.803 + 111.601) ÷ 2) = 75.42%.

StockAnalysis reports current TTM ROE of 114.29% and ROA of 52.73%. The difference is greater than 1% because the vendor uses a different current-period/denominator convention; it is not treated as a contradiction to the SEC-derived FY26 ratios. Both methods show exceptional capital efficiency, but the reported Q1 net income includes large investment gains, so operating ROIC should be tracked separately from GAAP ROE.

5. Competition and custom ASIC alternatives

Alternative Economic threat to NVIDIA Current investability comparison
AMD accelerators and CPUs More merchant-silicon choice; could pressure GPU ASPs and share, especially where software portability improves AMD trades at 181.55x trailing P/E and 102.55x EV/FCF despite much lower margins, reflecting high expectations
Broadcom custom accelerators and networking Custom silicon can win fixed, high-volume inference workloads; Broadcom also captures networking value Broadcom trades at 62.94x P/E and 57.32x EV/FCF, with 44.17% operating margin but substantial debt
Marvell custom silicon and networking Design wins can displace merchant chips in narrow workloads; execution and customer concentration matter Marvell trades at 71.83x P/E and 110.22x EV/FCF, with 16.41% operating margin and net debt
Hyperscaler ASICs Google TPU, AWS Trainium/Inferentia, Microsoft Maia, and Meta MTIA improve internal cost per token and bargaining power No standalone P/E or FCF; value is embedded in hyperscaler economics. They are most threatening in repeated, standardized inference rather than the entire CUDA ecosystem

NVIDIA’s own FY26 filing says it launched NVLink Fusion to let hyperscalers and custom ASIC designers integrate custom CPUs and XPUs with NVIDIA’s platform. That is strategically smart: NVIDIA can remain the system and networking standard even if some compute silicon becomes custom. But it also confirms that custom silicon is part of the competitive landscape, not a theoretical risk. Google describes TPU 8t/8i as custom accelerators for training and inference; Microsoft describes Maia 200 as an inference accelerator; Meta is developing multiple MTIA generations for recommendation and GenAI inference. See Google TPU 8th generation, Microsoft Maia 200, and Meta MTIA.

The right Buffett question is not “Can an ASIC beat a GPU on one benchmark?” It is “Can customers reduce total cost of ownership enough, across software, deployment, networking, utilization, and supply, to make switching worthwhile?” The risk is rising, but the burden of proof remains high.

NVIDIA is cheaper than AMD, Broadcom, and Marvell on trailing P/E and EV/FCF, while its margins and returns are materially stronger. That does not make it obviously cheap: a 42x FCF multiple still capitalizes a very long duration of high growth.

NVIDIA’s own valuation history from StockAnalysis ratios shows current P/E of 31.75x versus FY26 38.30x, FY25 48.51x, FY24 51.29x, FY23 119.77x, and FY22 59.32x. Current P/S of 19.81x is below FY26 21.12x and FY25 26.75x but above FY23 18.61x. Current EV/FCF of 41.82x is below FY26 46.65x and FY25 56.82x but still very high in absolute terms.

Exact market-cap check using rounded disclosed shares:

$207.29 × 24.22B shares = $5,020.5638B, versus StockAnalysis’ $5,020.673B; the difference is 0.0022%, comfortably below the 1% threshold and attributable to rounded shares/market data.

Exact multiples from rounded TTM values:

  • P/E = $5,020.673B ÷ $159.613B = 31.455x; the vendor’s 31.75x uses a different share/EPS timing convention.
  • P/S = $5,020.673B ÷ $253.491B = 19.806x.
  • EV/Sales = $4,980.315B ÷ $253.491B = 19.647x.
  • P/FCF = $5,020.673B ÷ $119.076B = 42.164x.
  • FCF yield = $119.076B ÷ $5,020.673B = 2.372%.

Reverse DCF

Using TTM FCF of $119.076B, conservative net cash of $68.224B, 24.22B shares, a 9% discount rate, and a 3% terminal growth rate, the July 21 market capitalization implies approximately 21.36% annual FCF growth for five years. That is the key valuation fact. The market is not merely pricing a good business; it is pricing sustained high growth at enormous scale.

Three-scenario intrinsic value

Formula:

Equity value = Σ[t=1..5] FCF0 × (1+g)^t ÷ (1+r)^t + FCF0 × (1+g)^6 ÷ ((r−terminal g) × (1+r)^5) + net cash

Shares are held at 24.22B; non-marketable investments receive zero value; buyback benefits are not separately credited.

Scenario FCF CAGR, years 1–5 Discount rate Terminal growth Year-5 FCF Intrinsic value/share Vs. $207.29
Bear 10% 10% 2.5% $191.773B $99.51 −52.0%
Base 20% 9% 3.0% $296.299B $194.94 −5.96%
Bull 30% 8% 3.5% $442.121B $406.08 +95.9%

Probability-weighted value at 25% bear / 50% base / 25% bull is $223.87/share, but Buffett-style underwriting should not rely on a favorable probability average when the downside case is a permanent impairment of capital. Applying a 25% margin of safety to the base value gives an entry price of $194.94 × 75% = $146.21. Applying a 35% margin of safety gives $126.71.

7. Bear, base, and bull operating cases

Bear case

AI data-center capex normalizes sharply; inference shifts toward TPUs and custom ASICs; AMD becomes good enough for more workloads; export controls limit China; inventory provisions recur; gross margin normalizes toward 65–68%; FCF grows only 10% annually. The DCF value is about $100/share.

Base case

AI infrastructure remains a large secular buildout, but growth decelerates from current extremes. Blackwell, Rubin, networking, sovereign AI, and enterprise software support roughly 20% annual FCF growth; gross margin normalizes toward 68–71%; operating margin remains above 55%; customer concentration is manageable; custom silicon takes share in selected inference workloads but NVIDIA retains the majority of general-purpose AI platform economics. The DCF value is about $195/share.

Bull case

Agentic AI, reasoning, physical AI, sovereign infrastructure, and inference demand expand the market faster than expected. Rubin and successor platforms preserve performance-per-dollar leadership; networking attach rates rise; software monetization improves; FCF compounds 30% annually and margins remain near current levels. The DCF value is about $406/share.

8. Inverse thinking: failure paths and indicators

Failure path Probability over five years Impact Observable indicator
Hyperscaler capex pause or poor AI returns Medium High Customer capex guidance cuts; lower utilization; delayed deployments
Custom ASIC adoption Medium-high High Two consecutive quarters of slower Compute growth than total AI infrastructure growth; lower networking attach
Gross-margin normalization Medium High GAAP gross margin below 68% for two quarters excluding one-time charges
Inventory and purchase-obligation mismatch Medium Medium-high Inventory growth persistently exceeds revenue growth; provisions recur above 2% of revenue
Customer concentration / payment-term stress Medium High Top three direct customers remain above 50%; receivables grow faster than revenue; extended terms increase
Export controls and geopolitics High Medium-high China revenue restrictions, licensing delays, or Taiwan supply disruptions
Key-person or execution failure Low-medium High Product delays, senior engineering departures, slower release cadence

The strongest bear argument is: NVIDIA’s accounting is real, but its current valuation assumes that a cyclical, capital-intensive customer ecosystem will sustain software-like growth and margins after customers have every incentive to lower their compute bill with in-house silicon. The most likely analytical mistake is confusing NVIDIA’s excellent current economics with proof that the same economics will persist at a $5T scale.

9. Final decision and action list

Dimension Conclusion Confidence
Business quality Exceptional platform business with real customer value and high returns High
Moat Very strong today; broadening through software, networking, and systems, but inference is the test Medium-high
Management Jensen Huang is highly aligned and has executed exceptional product/capital allocation decisions; succession remains a risk Medium-high
Balance sheet Fortress-like liquidity, low leverage, strong FCF; strategic equity investments add complexity High
Main risk Duration risk: AI demand, customer ROI, custom silicon, and margin durability High
Valuation Current price is below recent historical P/E but not cheap on FCF; base DCF is slightly below price High

Overall score: 3/5 stars. NVIDIA is a five-star business at a two-to-three-star price. The stock is not a short, but it is not a Buffett-style bargain at $207.29.

Investor Action
Empty-handed Wait for a better entry; consider only a small starter position if portfolio exposure is intentionally growth-heavy
Existing holder Hold if position size is disciplined; do not chase. Trim if NVIDIA has become an outsized portfolio concentration
Add zone Below $146/share for a 25% margin of safety to the base case; below $127 is a stronger margin-of-safety zone
Re-rating trigger Sustained FCF growth above 25%, gross margin above 72%, and evidence that custom ASICs increase NVIDIA networking/platform attach rather than displace it
Thesis-break trigger Two quarters of sub-20% Data Center growth with gross margin below 68%, worsening receivables/inventory, or clear customer migration to non-NVIDIA stacks

Buffett-style conclusion: “Wonderful company” is not the same as “wonderful investment.” At the current price, I would rather own NVIDIA than most semiconductor peers, but I would prefer to buy it below $146 and would keep enough cash available for the next semiconductor or AI-capex disappointment.

AI analysis confidence versus investment certainty

AI analysis confidence: high for historical data and current disclosures. FY24–FY26 SEC numbers reconcile to StockAnalysis with 0.00% differences for the principal income statement and cash-flow items; current valuation data is dated July 21, 2026. The main source limitation is that Macrotrends was blocked by HTTP 403 and the local financial-rigor/audit scripts were unavailable.

Investment certainty: medium. NVIDIA’s moat, balance sheet, and current cash generation are unusually observable. The uncertain variables are future customer returns on AI infrastructure, custom ASIC penetration, the persistence of current gross margins, export controls, and whether AI inference demand expands faster than unit economics compress. High data confidence should not be mistaken for high certainty about a five-year intrinsic value.
